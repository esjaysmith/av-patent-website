# The Journey Behind US Patent 12,001,207

## A Vision for the Future of Autonomous Navigation

When we began developing vision-based autonomous navigation technology in the mid-2010s, autonomous vehicles were largely a futuristic concept for most people. Our patent (US 12,001,207, priority date August 2019, issued June 2024) emerged from years of research into end-to-end neural network approaches. Today, the landscape has transformed dramatically—robotaxis and autonomous delivery vehicles are becoming reality, and large-scale deployment of self-driving technology is accelerating with advancements like Tesla's FSD v12.

## Our Technical Conviction: Vision-Based End-to-End Learning

From the outset, our conviction was clear: **effective autonomous vehicles must rely on vision-based, end-to-end deep learning**. This approach diverged significantly from the industry standard at the time, which favored lidar-driven, sensor-heavy systems with detailed mapping and object classification.

We asked ourselves a fundamental question:

> How should an autonomous vehicle navigate, stay on its intended path, and avoid obstacles using only vision—without relying on overly detailed maps that must be constantly updated?

This question drove our research, development, and ultimately, our patent applications. We sought to protect not just our intellectual property, but the investments and innovations that emerged from years of focused development.

## Validation Through Industry Evolution

The evolution of Tesla's FSD v12 to exclusively use camera-fed neural networks trained end-to-end has validated our early technical conviction. When we compare this approach to the challenges faced by other players in the autonomous vehicle space—such as Cruise's difficulties that led to their shutdown in late 2024 after significant investment—we see compelling evidence for vision-first architectures.

## Our Technical Achievement

### End-to-End Neural Network Architecture

Our technology demonstrates vision-based autonomous navigation in action. The system we developed features:

**Core Innovation:**
- **End-to-end training**: Images in, vehicle controls out
- **Visual navigation**: Direction determination based on visual waypoints
- **Map-independent operation**: No reliance on detailed mapping data
- **Obstacle avoidance**: Free passage detection and uncertainty-based stopping

**Architectural Foundation:**
- Imitation learning foundation (building on NVIDIA DAVE-2, 2016)
- Path planning through visual prompting (proprietary method)
- Free passage detection and model uncertainty (proprietary method)
- Upside-Down Reinforcement Learning integration (Schmidhuber et al., 2019-2022)

**System Capabilities:**
- Real-time autonomous navigation using only camera inputs
- Adaptive decision-making based on environmental uncertainty
- Remote teleoperation and data collection capabilities
- Continuous learning from operational data

Our technology in operation can be viewed here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SOWuOP-0nDE

The demonstration shows our autonomous research vehicle navigating independently using vision-based controls, with real-time path visualization and obstacle awareness.

## Why This Patent Is Available for Licensing

### The Regulatory Reality

Our self-funded startup successfully established a business case in a rural area, securing customer commitments and local regulatory approvals. However, we encountered challenges obtaining final regulatory approval from the central government in the Netherlands. These challenges stemmed from our organization's limited size and resource constraints.

### The Scale Challenge

Deep learning AI requires substantial data collection and computational resources—significantly more than initially anticipated. While we've demonstrated the technical viability of our approach, the investment required to achieve commercial scale exceeds our organizational capacity.

**The reality we faced:**
- Regulatory approval processes favor larger, well-funded organizations
- Modern AI systems require data collection at scale
- Neural network training demands significant computational infrastructure
- Achieving generalization across diverse environments requires extensive real-world testing

The combination of regulatory hurdles and scale requirements made continuing independent development unfeasible.
